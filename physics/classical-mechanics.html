Newtonian Mechanics

The foundation of classical physics based on Newton's three laws

Newton's Three Laws of Motion

1

Law of Inertia

1687

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force.

\( \sum \vec{F} = 0 \Rightarrow \vec{v} = \text{constant} \)
Seatbelts Ice Hockey
2

F = ma

Fundamental Equation

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.

\( \sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a} \)
Rocket Launch Car Crashes
3

Action-Reaction

Equal & Opposite

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. Forces always occur in pairs between two interacting bodies.

\( \vec{F}_{AB} = -\vec{F}_{BA} \)
Walking Rocket Propulsion

Conservation Laws

Conservation of Energy

In a closed system, the total energy remains constant over time. Energy can neither be created nor destroyed, only transformed.

\( E_{\text{total}} = T + V = \text{constant} \)
Types of Energy:
Kinetic Energy: \( T = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \)
Potential Energy: \( V = mgh \)
Thermal Energy

Conservation of Momentum

In the absence of external forces, the total momentum of a system remains constant. This applies to both linear and angular momentum.

\( \sum \vec{p} = \sum m\vec{v} = \text{constant} \)
Applications:
Collision Analysis
Rocket Equations
Rotational Systems

Work-Energy Theorem

The work done by all forces acting on a particle equals the change in the particle's kinetic energy. This provides a powerful alternative to Newton's second law for solving mechanics problems.

\( W = \Delta T = T_f - T_i \)

Work Calculation

Constant Force: \( W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{d} \)
Variable Force: \( W = \int \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} \)
Power: \( P = \frac{dW}{dt} \)

Oscillations & Waves

Periodic motion and wave phenomena in classical systems

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

SHM occurs when the restoring force is directly proportional to the displacement and acts in the direction opposite to that of displacement. This leads to sinusoidal motion.

\( x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi) \)
\( v(t) = -A\omega\sin(\omega t + \phi) \)
\( a(t) = -A\omega^2\cos(\omega t + \phi) \)

Types of Oscillations

Undamped

Perfect harmonic motion with constant amplitude. No energy loss to surroundings.

\( \ddot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = 0 \)
Ideal Spring

Damped

Energy dissipates due to friction or resistance. Amplitude decreases exponentially.

\( \ddot{x} + 2\beta\dot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = 0 \)
Shock Absorbers

Forced

External periodic force drives the system. Can lead to resonance.

\( \ddot{x} + 2\beta\dot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = F_0\cos(\omega t) \)

Orbital Mechanics

Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion

First Law

Planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun at one focus.

\( r = \frac{a(1-e^2)}{1+e\cos\theta} \)
Elliptical orbits

Second Law

A line joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal times.

\( \frac{dA}{dt} = \text{constant} \)
Angular momentum conservation

Third Law

The square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis.

\( T^2 \propto a^3 \)

Historical Timeline

The evolution of classical mechanics from ancient times to modern formulations

Ancient Greece (4th Century BC)

Aristotle, Archimedes

Aristotle's physics: Natural motion vs violent motion. Objects fall at speeds proportional to their weight. Archimedes' principle of buoyancy and levers. "Give me a lever long enough and a fulcrum on which to place it, and I shall move the world."

Natural Philosophy Levers & Buoyancy

1632-1638

Galileo Galilei

"Dialogues Concerning Two New Sciences" - Established the law of falling bodies. All objects fall at the same rate regardless of mass (ignoring air resistance). Introduced the concept of inertia and the principle of relativity.

Law of Falling Bodies Inertia Principle
