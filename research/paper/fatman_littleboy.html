Technical Specifications

Little Boy

Gun-Type Design
Target: Hiroshima
Date: August 6, 1945
Yield: 15 kilotons
Fissile Material: Uranium-235 (64 kg)
Length: 3.0 meters
Diameter: 0.71 meters
Weight: 4,400 kg

Design Principle:

Gun-type assembly. One sub-critical mass of U-235 fired into another using conventional explosives. Simple but inefficient design with only 1.7% of material undergoing fission.

Fat Man

Implosion Design
Target: Nagasaki
Date: August 9, 1945
Yield: 21 kilotons
Fissile Material: Plutonium-239 (6.2 kg)
Length: 3.25 meters
Diameter: 1.52 meters
Weight: 4,670 kg

Design Principle:

Implosion assembly. High explosives compress sub-critical plutonium sphere to super-critical density. More efficient design with neutron initiator at center.

Trinity Test - First Nuclear Explosion

The world's first nuclear weapon test conducted on July 16, 1945, at Alamogordo, New Mexico. This marked the beginning of the nuclear age.

Trinity Test Details

Date:

July 16, 1945

Location:

Alamogordo, New Mexico

Yield:

~25 kilotons

Significance:

First detonation of a nuclear weapon using Fat Man's implosion design with plutonium core.

Key Observations

  • Fireball reached 600 meters in diameter within 2 seconds
  • Mushroom cloud rose to 12,000 meters altitude
  • Ground temperature: 8,430°C (15,200°F) at hypocenter
  • Sand fused into green glass (trinitite)

Historical Quote

Nuclear Physics & Design Details

Little Boy: Gun-Type Design

Components:

1

Target Assembly

38 kg U-235 rings (sub-critical mass)

2

Projectile Assembly

26 kg U-235 cylinder (sub-critical mass)

3

Gun Barrel

1.8m long, 10cm diameter steel tube

Firing Sequence:

  1. Cordite explosive propellant ignited
  2. Projectile accelerated to 300 m/s down barrel
  3. Projectile strikes target assembly
  4. Super-critical mass achieved (64 kg total)
  5. Neutron initiator activated
  6. Chain reaction begins within 1 millisecond

Fat Man: Implosion Design

Layered Structure:

1

Plutonium Core

6.2 kg Pu-239 sphere (sub-critical)

2

Tamper/Reflector

Natural uranium shell (120 kg)

3

Explosive Lenses

32 pentagonal/hexagonal segments

Implosion Sequence:

  1. Detonators fire simultaneously (within 0.1 μs)
  2. Explosive shockwave converges spherically
  3. Plutonium core compressed to 2× density
  4. Neutron initiator crushed, releasing neutrons
  5. Super-critical state achieved
  6. Chain reaction occurs in ~1 μs

Key Technical Differences

Little Boy Advantages:

  • Simple, reliable design
  • Didn't require testing (used directly)
  • Less sensitive to manufacturing defects
  • Faster to assemble in field

Fat Man Advantages:

  • Higher efficiency (14% vs 1.38%)
  • Less fissile material required
  • Smaller critical mass possible
  • Better for implosion-type hydrogen bombs

Scientific & Political Consequences

Scientific Advances:

  • Development of nuclear energy for peaceful purposes
  • Advances in particle physics and materials science
  • Creation of nuclear medicine (radiation therapy)
  • Improved understanding of radiation effects

Political Changes:

  • Beginning of the Cold War and nuclear arms race
  • Establishment of the United Nations and IAEA
  • Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT) of 1968
  • Doctrine of Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD)

Ethical Considerations

The bombings remain one of the most debated ethical decisions in military history, raising questions about proportionality, civilian casualties, and the morality of using weapons of mass destruction.
